## Section — BACKGROUND

Narrative prose (separate from the tabulated Vessel's Movements / Events chronology).
Scope: sets the context of the claim — who the vessel is, what it is generally
employed to do, and the specific activity / voyage it was engaged in leading up
to (but stopping just before) the incident. The occurrence itself belongs in the
following Damage / Occurrence narrative, not here.

Optional opening — source attribution (include only when the account is drawn from an owner / third-party investigation report; omit when it is the surveyor's own knowledge):

The following background is a summary of the description of events in the [SOURCE_REPORT] (Annex: [X]). Further background information is listed as known by the Undersigned Surveyor.

Context body, in order:

1. Vessel's general role / employment. What the vessel is and what it is ordinarily engaged in.
2. The specific operation or voyage in progress. On [DATE], the [VESSEL] was engaged in [OPERATION] / had departed [PORT] on a voyage between [A] and [B], carrying out [ACTIVITY].
3. Immediate circumstances up to just before the incident. Prevailing conditions, what the vessel/crew were doing, ending at the point immediately preceding the occurrence.

## Section — OCCURRENCE NARRATIVE (writing rules)

Sourcing rule: open with the attribution "It was reported by the vessel's Master
that ..." (or Chief Engineer / owner's representative, as applicable). The whole
event account is the reported version, not the surveyor's — keep it in reported
register throughout. The surveyor's own findings come later, in Damage Description.

Data-capture rule: this section must consume EVERY hard data point and context
cue available before any prose is written. Inventory the following first and work
them all in — do not summarise past them:
- Exact dates and clock times (use as given, e.g. "At 1719 hours"), and named
  time zones where stated.
- Positions (lat/long), locations, port/berth/anchorage names, waypoints, distances.
- Named persons by rank/role (Master, Chief Engineer, C/O, superintendent, contractor).
- Named equipment/systems and their state (alarms, valves, thrusters, tow wire,
  lengths, catenary, speeds, headings), plus readings/quantities.
- Weather and sea state, and whether actual vs forecast.
- Every alarm, its label, time, and the crew's response to it.
- Any secondary/cascading failure and its sequence.
- Notifications made (managers, AMSA, ERT, third parties) with times/reference numbers.
Draw from the Chronology/Movements table, incident reports, investigation reports,
class statements and logs. Anything present must appear; if a hard fact is absent,
write "Not confirmed" rather than inventing or glossing.

Three-act structure:

Act 1 — Prelude (all normal). Set the operating state immediately before the event:
who was on watch, what operation was underway, conditions, systems running normally.
Anchor with time and place. ("It was reported by the Master that at [TIME] on [DATE],
the vessel was [OPERATION] at [LOCATION], with [conditions/systems state].")

Act 2 — The event (the drama), in tick-tock sequence. The first sign (noise, alarm,
shudder, level) with its exact time; the crew member who detected it and what they
did; what was then found; each cascading failure in order with its own time; loss of
function; the point the situation was contained or the casualty completed. Keep every
alarm and each response as a discrete, timed step.

Act 3 — Aftermath / response, through to stabilisation. Notification up the chain
(Chief, managers, ERT, AMSA, P&I) with times/refs; immediate mitigation (pumping,
isolating, shadowing, third-party tow); the voyage/tow to a place of safety;
initial crew or contractor/diver inspection carried out en route or in port — this
is where preliminary works and first damage assessment get captured; and the endpoint
that hands over to repairs / dry-docking. This act naturally feeds the Damage
Description (surveyor's own inspection) that follows.

Register note: keep Acts 1-3 attributed/reported ("was reported", "it was found",
"the crew observed"). Switch to the surveyor's own voice only once Damage Description
begins.

## Section — DAMAGE DESCRIPTION

Damage as evidenced observation.

Data-capture rule: much of the damage source material is structured — hard fields
and object-oriented records (component registers, diver/class findings tables,
compartment matrices, service reports, alarm/telemetry readings, photo captions).
Every such field must be consumed and carried into this section before prose is
written. Do not summarise past a field or collapse a per-object record into a
generic statement. Inventory and use all of the following where present:
- Each named object/component and its identifier (tag, frame/compartment No.,
  tank ID, valve/actuator No., serial No., position P/C/S).
- The condition/mode of damage for that specific object (crack, set-in, hole,
  water ingress, corrosion, deformation, missing, abraded, immersed, inoperable).
- All measurements and quantities exactly as recorded (dimensions, depth of
  indentation, crack length, hours, count of items affected, ratings/scales).
- The state qualifier per object (e.g. "cover removed", "fully immersed",
  "out of water at time of inspection", "no visible damage").
- The evidentiary source for each block (surveyor's own inspection, diver report,
  class survey, service report) and its date.
- Photo captions and any reference-table ratings (e.g. paint/fouling scales),
  reproduced against the object they describe.
Where a field exists in the source but has no value, write "Not confirmed" rather
than omitting the object. The aim: a reader can reconstruct the full damaged-object
inventory from this section alone.

Structure:

1. State the basis of the findings first (this sets the evidentiary weight). Use the opener that matches how damage was established:
   - Direct inspection: The [COMPONENT] was inspected [at/on] [FACILITY] on [DATE]. [Condition on arrival, e.g. cover removed]. The following damage was observed:
   - Remote / reported: A [telephone discussion / meeting] was held with [NAMES] on [DATE]. It was established that the following areas of damage existed:
   - Third-party survey: The following damage was reported from the [Diving Survey / Class survey]:
2. Bulleted findings — one per line, each naming the object/component + its identifier + condition/mode of damage + measurements (plain, factual, not prose). One bullet per damaged object; do not merge objects.
3. Consequential / suspected damage — It is considered that there could have been further damage to [COMPONENTS] due to [reason].

For extensive structural cases: break into area sub-headings (e.g. Hull Damage, Deck Fittings, Equipment Stowed on Deck), incorporate/extract the class or diver report, and use a compartment matrix where useful (rows = frame/compartment, columns = Stbd / Centre / Port) with captioned photos — preserving the per-cell object data.

## Section — CAUSE CONSIDERATION

The surveyor's reasoned opinion on how the damage arose. This is the surveyor's
own voice (not a reported account) and must be tied back to the physical evidence
already recorded in Damage Description.

Evidence-linkage rule: every causal statement must be anchored to a specific
observed finding or piece of source evidence. Do not assert a cause that isn't
traceable to the damage record, a third-party report, or a stated fact. Consume
all available causal inputs before writing:
- Each damage finding/object from Damage Description and the failure mode it implies
  (fracture = shock load, corrosion = water ingress, abrasion = contact, etc.).
- Owner / crew account of the event and any stated allegation of cause.
- Third-party investigation findings (5 Whys, RCA, class, service reports) with source ref.
- Physical measurements, clearances, oil analysis, photos, and any absent-evidence
  points (e.g. "measurements weren't taken") that limit certainty.
- Any conflict between sources, which the surveyor should call out rather than smooth over.
Where causation cannot be firmly established, say so and state what further evidence
(docking, disassembly, independent inspection) is needed — do not overstate.

Structure:

1. Damage-to-mechanism link. State what the observed damage is consistent with:
   The [damage] to [object] is consistent with [mechanism/load], arising from [cause].
2. Reasoning across the evidence. Work through contributing findings, third-party
   findings (with ref), and any sequence (e.g. secondary damage from a later action).
3. Treatment of uncertainty / conflicts. Note gaps, untaken measurements, or
   disagreements between reports; give preliminary vs final standing where relevant.
4. Concluding opinion. A single clear surveyor's view:
   - Firm: It is the opinion of the Undersigned Surveyor that [the incident was caused by ...].
   - Agreeing with a third party: The surveyor agrees that [cause] would be the likely cause.
   - Preliminary: A final conclusion cannot be reached until [condition]. On a preliminary basis, the following potential causes are considered: [list].

Registers seen (pick per certainty level): full reasoned analysis; short agreement
with an owner/third-party finding; or a preliminary "potential causes" list pending
further inspection.

## GLOBAL RULE — HEDGING / CALIBRATED LANGUAGE

Applies to the whole report, most critically to Cause Consideration, Damage
Description (suspected damage) and Closing Comments. The goal is calibrated
confidence, not blanket qualification: state firmly what was directly observed,
hedge everything that is inferred, reported, or predicted. Over-hedging is a fault
too — it makes the report unreadable and dilutes the findings that ARE firm.

Core principle — match the verb to the evidence:
- Directly observed by the surveyor -> state as fact, no hedge.
  ("The top cover had been removed." "A tooth had broken away from the bull gear.")
- Reported by others / not personally witnessed -> attribute, don't adopt.
  ("It was reported that ...", "The Master advised that ...", "reportedly".)
- Inferred cause / mechanism -> hedge with a calibrated term.
  ("is consistent with", "would be", "likely", "appears to have", "considered to be".)
- Predicted / future / unverified -> hedge and state the dependency.
  ("could foreseeably", "would be unlikely", "pending inspection at docking".)

Hedge one clause, not every clause: apply a single calibrated qualifier to the
inference itself, then let the supporting facts stand unhedged. Do NOT stack
hedges ("it may possibly be considered that it could perhaps..."). One hedge per
proposition is enough.

Approved calibrated terms (roughly ascending confidence), for variety and to
avoid repeating one word:
- Low: it is possible that; may have; could have; cannot be excluded.
- Medium: appears to; is consistent with; suggests; likely; on balance.
- High (still short of certainty): strongly indicates; the evidence points to;
  in the opinion of the Undersigned Surveyor.
Reserve "is consistent with" for the damage-to-mechanism link — it is the workhorse.

Attribution carries hedging on its own: once a statement is attributed ("the
Master reported"), it does not also need "possibly" — the attribution already
signals it is unverified.

Certainty ladder for conclusions (choose the rung the evidence supports):
- Preliminary: A final conclusion cannot be reached until [condition]. On a
  preliminary basis, the following potential causes are considered ...
- Qualified opinion: On balance, and on the basis of the evidence available, it is
  the opinion of the Undersigned Surveyor that [cause] is the likely cause.
- Firm opinion (only where evidence is strong): It is the opinion of the
  Undersigned Surveyor that [cause].
Even a firm opinion is framed as the surveyor's opinion, never as absolute fact.

Absent-evidence flag: where a fact could not be verified or a measurement was not
taken, say so plainly ("measurements were not taken", "not confirmed") — this both
hedges the conclusion and protects the surveyor.

Without-prejudice framing: findings and approved costs are noted Without Prejudice
to Underwriters' liability where appropriate; the report does not determine liability.

Readability guard: aim for hedges on the inference-bearing sentences only. A
paragraph of observed facts should read cleanly; the hedge appears at the point a
conclusion is drawn from them. If a sentence has two qualifiers, delete one.

## Section — REPAIR TIMES

Do NOT state a single overall total. The loss adjuster works per repair period,
and within each period the time is further split by occurrence (claim), then by
average (casualty-related) vs Owners' own work. Structure the section to reflect
that nesting; use only the levels that actually apply to the case.

Nesting: Repair Period -> Occurrence (claim) -> Damage/average vs Owners' repairs.
- Single period, single occurrence: one simple split (average vs Owners').
- Multiple periods: report each period separately; do not sum across periods.
- Multiple occurrences within a period: split that period's time by occurrence.

Generic introduction (period-scoped, hypothetical-separation basis — the wording
that lets the adjuster apportion time). Adapt per period; drop rows/columns that
don't apply:

For the guidance of those concerned, it is our opinion that had the repairs [in
this period / at [PORT/YARD]] been carried out separately, the following periods
would have been required:

Table columns as applicable: In Dry Dock | Afloat (and only add Total per row if
useful for a single-occurrence period — never a grand total across periods).
Row labels use the average/owner split:
- Damage repairs (average) — casualty-related work, per occurrence (e.g. "Occurrence No. 1", "Occurrence No. 2").
- Owners' repairs / Work not concerning average — Owners' own concurrent work.

Owners' own work — introduce its scope separately where present:

Concurrently with the average repairs, the Owners / Managers of the vessel
instructed repairs to be carried out to their own account. These included, but
were not limited to, the following works: [bulleted list].

Hedging: keep the opinion framing ("it is our opinion that ... would have been
required") — these are estimated separated durations, not recorded fact.

## Note — CHRONOLOGY TABLE PLACEMENT

The main date table (Chronology of Events / Vessel's Movements) normally sits
between Available Information Sources and Background. It can alternatively be
moved to just after the Class & Statutory Certification section (5). Either
placement is acceptable; it must remain immediately ahead of the Background so
the tabular skeleton precedes the narrative that expands it.

## CONVENTION — ITALIC PURPOSE LINE UNDER EACH SECTION TITLE

Immediately beneath every section heading, insert a single italic sentence stating
the purpose of that section, before any content. It orients the surveyor and gives
the AI an explicit intent anchor for what the section must achieve.

Style rules:
- One sentence, italicised, present tense, starting "This section ...".
- Concise (roughly 12-25 words); describes intent, not content or instructions.
- Neutral, professional register; no hedging needed (it is a statement of purpose).

Suggested purpose lines per section:

- Introduction / Scope of Work — *This section sets out who instructed the survey, when and where the vessel was attended, and the purpose of the attendance.*
- Attending Representatives — *This section records the persons present at the survey and meetings and the party each represented.*
- Vessel's Particulars — *This section identifies the subject vessel and its principal characteristics.*
- Brief Technical Description — *This section describes the vessel and, where relevant, the particulars of the machinery forming the subject of the claim.*
- Class & Statutory Certification — *This section evaluates the classification and statutory certification status of the subject vessel.*
- Chronology of Events / Vessel's Movements — *This section presents the dated sequence of movements and events relevant to the casualty.*
- Background — *This section sets out the vessel's employment and the operation or voyage in progress leading up to the incident.*
- Occurrence Narrative — *This section records, as reported, the circumstances of the incident from the prevailing conditions through to the vessel reaching a place of safety.*
- Damage Description — *This section sets out the damage established on inspection, identifying each affected component and its condition.*
- Cause Consideration — *This section provides the surveyor's reasoned opinion as to how the damage arose, on the basis of the available evidence.*
- Repairs — *This section describes the temporary and permanent repairs carried out or proposed.*
- Repair Times — *This section estimates, per repair period, the time attributable to average repairs and to the Owners' own work.*
- Repair Cost — *This section assesses the costs presented in respect of the casualty-related repairs.*
- Waiver — *This section sets out the basis and limitations on which this report is issued.*

Adapt the wording to the actual section title used in the report.
